\subsection*{The Leverage of Middle Management}
Perhaps counter-intuitively, the highest distinction scores in a strict hierarchy are not held by the root node, but rather by the middle branches (Nodes 2 and 3). 
While the middle branches share the maximum status ($s_i = 1.0$) with the root node due to their capacity to connect the top of the hierarchy to the leaves, they experience substantially less constraint ($\kappa_i = 0.1925$). If a middle branch node is deleted, its pendant leaves become structurally isolated components, dropping their status to zero, while the root node loses half of its influence. Therefore, middle managers possess immense structural leverage: their structural ``exit'' destroys the status of their dependents rather than empowering them. 

\subsection*{A Simplified Expression for the Root Node}
The root node (Node 1) is penalized with a negative distinction score. This formalizes a core sociological intuition common to theories of agonistic competition: a pure autocrat is maximally constrained because they represent a single point of failure for the entire system. 

Under the absolute maximum normalization, we can derive a simplified, exact mathematical expression for the scaled distinction of the root node in any balanced hierarchical tree.

First, because the root is maximally central to the entire connected hierarchy, its initial normalized status is equal to one:
\begin{equation}
    s_{root} = 1
\end{equation}

Second, the structural constraint ($\kappa_i$) is calculated as the average status of the focal node's alters in the $N-1$ subgraph where the focal node is deleted. If the root node of a balanced tree is removed, the graph shatters into disconnected but structurally identical sub-trees. The former direct children of the root now serve as the supreme ``roots'' of their respective disconnected components. Because these components are identical, each of the former children ties for the maximum eigenvector centrality in the residual network. Under \texttt{abm} normalization, their status scores all become exactly $1$. Thus, the average status of the root's alters in the deleted subgraph is $1$:
\begin{equation}
    \kappa_{root} = \frac{1}{|N(root)|} \sum_{j \in N(root)} s_{j}^{(deleted)} = 1
\end{equation}

Substituting these two constants into the fundamental equation for scaled distinction ($\beta_i = \alpha s_i - \kappa_i$), the expression simplifies entirely to a function of the global network scalar:

\begin{equation}
    \beta_{root} = \alpha(1) - 1 = \alpha - 1
\end{equation}

Because the global scalar $\alpha$ is defined as the network's ratio of average constraint to average status ($\bar{\kappa} / \bar{s}$), and is practically always less than 1 in structured networks, this mathematical proof guarantees that the single root node of a strict hierarchy will \textit{always} possess negative distinction. Their dominance is highly precarious: deleting the root immediately catapults all of their direct subordinates to the maximum possible structural status in the resulting fragmented system.
